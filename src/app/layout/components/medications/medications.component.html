1-Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses anti cancer (cytotoxic) drugs to destroy brain tumour cells. The drugs circulate throughout your body in the bloodstream.

You might have chemotherapy after surgery or if your brain tumour comes back. Common chemotherapy drugs for brain tumours are a drug called temozolomide. And a combination of drugs called procarbazine, lomustine and vincristine (PCV).

It can be difficult to treat brain tumours with some chemotherapy drugs because the brain is protected by the blood brain barrier. This is a natural filter between the blood and the brain which protects the brain from harmful substances.

Chemotherapy drugs

Chemotherapy drugs are powerful medications used to treat cancer by targeting and killing rapidly dividing cancer cells. They are commonly used in the treatment of various types of cancer, including brain tumors. Chemotherapy can be given alone or in combination with other treatments such as surgery, radiation therapy, or immunotherapy. Here's some general information about chemotherapy drugs:

1. How They Work:

Chemotherapy drugs work by interfering with the cell division process, preventing cancer cells from growing and multiplying. However, they can also affect healthy cells that divide rapidly, leading to side effects.

2. Administration:

Chemotherapy can be administered in various ways, including intravenous (IV) infusion, oral pills or capsules, intrathecal (directly into the cerebrospinal fluid), or as implantable wafers.

3. Treatment Cycles:

Chemotherapy is typically given in cycles, with a treatment period followed by a rest period to allow the body to recover from side effects.

4. Side Effects:

Common side effects of chemotherapy may include nausea, vomiting, fatigue, hair loss, decreased blood cell counts (leading to increased risk of infection and bleeding), and gastrointestinal issues. However, not all patients experience the same side effects, and they may vary depending on the specific drug and individual response.

5. Combination Therapy:

In brain tumor treatment, chemotherapy is often used in combination with other therapies to improve outcomes. It can be combined with radiation therapy, targeted therapies, or immunotherapies.

6. Personalized Treatment:

Each patient's treatment plan is individualized based on factors such as tumor type, stage, overall health, and other medical considerations. Not all brain tumors respond the same way to chemotherapy, and different drugs may be used for specific tumor types.

7. Monitoring and Follow-up:

During chemotherapy, patients are closely monitored for side effects and treatment effectiveness. Follow-up scans and tests are conducted to assess tumor response and make any necessary adjustments to the treatment plan.

Temozolomide

Temozolomide is an oral chemotherapy drug commonly used to treat high-grade gliomas, such as glioblastoma multiforme. It works by damaging the DNA of cancer cells, inhibiting their growth and division. Temozolomide is often used in combination with radiation therapy to treat brain tumors.

- Brand Name: Temodar

- Administration: Oral (capsules)

- Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, fatigue, hair loss, and lowered blood cell counts.

- Note: Temozolomide is often used as part of the standard treatment for glioblastoma multiforme and is known for its effectiveness against this aggressive brain tumor.

Carmustine (BCNU)

Carmustine, also known as BCNU, is an alkylating agent used for treating brain tumors, particularly gliomas. It is administered through implantable wafers or intravenously. Carmustine works by interfering with the DNA of cancer cells, preventing them from replicating and growing.

- Brand Name: BiCNU

- Administration: Implantable wafers or intravenous infusion

- Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, decreased appetite, and low blood cell counts.

- Note: Carmustine wafers are surgically implanted into the brain tumor cavity after tumor resection, delivering localized chemotherapy directly to the tumor site.

Lomustine (CCNU)

Lomustine, also known as CCNU, is another alkylating agent used to treat brain tumors. It is taken orally and can cross the blood-brain barrier, making it effective against brain cancers. Lomustine is often used in combination with other chemotherapy drugs for better results.

- Brand Name: Gleostine

- Administration: Oral (capsules)

- Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, delayed bone marrow suppression, and liver toxicity.

- Note: Lomustine is commonly used to treat high-grade gliomas and is sometimes combined with other chemotherapy drugs.

Vincristine

Vincristine is a vinca alkaloid chemotherapy drug used in the treatment of brain tumors, including medulloblastoma. It works by disrupting the formation of microtubules in cancer cells, hindering their ability to divide and spread.

- Brand Name: Vincasar PFS

- Administration: Intravenous infusion

- Side Effects: Peripheral neuropathy, constipation, and hair loss.

- Note: Vincristine is part of the standard treatment regimen for medulloblastoma and other pediatric brain tumors.

Methotrexate

Methotrexate is an antimetabolite chemotherapy drug used for brain tumors like primary central nervous system lymphoma. It interferes with the production of DNA and RNA in cancer cells, leading to their death. Methotrexate can be administered intravenously or intrathecally.

- Brand Name: Trexall, Rheumatrex

- Administration: Intravenous infusion or intrathecal injection (directly into the cerebrospinal fluid).

- Side Effects: Nausea, mouth sores, decreased blood cell counts, and liver function changes.

- Note: Methotrexate is used to treat primary central nervous system lymphoma and some other brain tumors.

Cisplatin

Cisplatin is a platinum-based chemotherapy drug used to treat various cancers, including some brain tumors. It works by damaging the DNA of cancer cells and preventing them from multiplying. Cisplatin is often used in combination with other chemotherapy drugs.

- Brand Name: Platinol

- Administration: Intravenous infusion

- Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, kidney damage, and nerve damage.

- Note: Cisplatin is commonly used in combination with radiation therapy for certain brain tumors.

Carboplatin

Carboplatin is another platinum-based chemotherapy drug used to treat brain tumors. It is similar to cisplatin in its mechanism of action but may have fewer side effects. Carboplatin is often used in combination with other treatments.

- Brand Name: Paraplatin

- Administration: Intravenous infusion

- Side Effects: Nausea, hair loss, and decreased blood cell counts.

- Note: Carboplatin is often used as an alternative to cisplatin in some cases due to its potentially lower toxicity.

Etoposide

Etoposide is a topoisomerase inhibitor chemotherapy drug used to treat certain brain tumors, such as germ cell tumors. It interferes with the enzyme topoisomerase, which is essential for DNA replication in cancer cells, causing their death.

- Brand Name: VePesid

- Administration: Intravenous infusion or oral capsules.

- Side Effects: Nausea, hair loss, and lowered blood cell counts.

- Note: Etoposide is used to treat germ cell tumors, including those that may occur in the brain.

2-Targeted Therapy

Targeted therapy is a type of cancer treatment that uses drugs or other substances to precisely identify and attack certain types of cancer cells. A targeted therapy can be used by itself or in combination with other treatments, such as traditional or standard chemotherapy, surgery, or radiation therapy. If your treatment plan includes targeted therapy, knowing how it works and what to expect can often help you prepare for treatment and make informed decisions about your care.

What is targeted therapy?

Targeted therapy is a type of cancer treatment that uses drugs designed to "target" cancer cells without affecting normal cells.Cancer cells typically have changes in their genes that make them different from normal cells. Genes are part of a cell's DNA that tell the cell to do certain things. When a cell has certain gene changes, it doesn't behave like a normal cell. For example, gene changes in cancer cells might allow the cell to grow and divide very quickly. These types of changes are what make it a cancer cell.

But there are many different types of cancer, and not all cancer cells are the same. For example, colon cancer and breast cancer cells have different gene changes that help them grow and/or spread. Even among different people with the same general type of cancer (such as colon cancer), the cancer cells can have different gene changes, making one person's specific type of colon cancer different from another person's.

Researchers have also learned that the environment in which different cancers start, grow, and thrive are not always the same. For example, some cancers have certain types of proteins or enzymes send certain messages to tell the cancer cell to grow and copy itself. Knowing these details has led to the development of drugs that can “target” these proteins or enzymes and block the messages being sent. Targeted drugs can block or turn off signals that make cancer cells grow, or can signal the cancer cells to destroy themselves.

Targeted therapy is a type of cancer treatment that specifically targets certain molecules or pathways that are involved in the growth and spread of cancer cells. Unlike traditional chemotherapy, which affects both cancerous and healthy cells that divide rapidly, targeted therapy is designed to be more selective and focused on cancer cells. Here's some information about targeted therapy:

1. How targeted therapy works?

Targeted therapy drugs can work in different ways. Some drugs may block the action of specific proteins that promote cancer growth, while others may interfere with the signals that cancer cells use to divide and grow. Some targeted therapies may also trigger the immune system to attack cancer cells.

Targeted therapies are made to find and attack specific areas or substances in cancer cells, or can detect and block certain kinds of messages sent inside a cancer cell that tell it to grow. Some of the substances in cancer cells that become the "targets" of targeted therapies are:

  • Too much of a certain protein on a cancer cell
  • A protein on a cancer cell that is not on normal cells
  • A protein that is mutated (changed) in some way on a cancer cell
  • Gene (DNA) changes that aren't in a normal cell.

The action of targeted drugs can work to:

  • Block or turn off chemical signals that tell the cancer cell to grow and divide
  • Change proteins within the cancer cells so the cells die
  • Stop making new blood vessels to feed the cancer cells
  • Trigger your immune system to kill the cancer cells
  • Carry toxins to the cancer cells to kill them, but not normal cells

The action of the drugs can affect where these drugs work and what side effects they cause.It's important to note that some targeted therapy drugs, for example, monoclonal antibodies, work in more than one way to control cancer cells and may also be considered immunotherapy because they boost the immune system.

2. Specific Molecular Targets:

Targeted therapy drugs are designed to interact with specific molecules that play critical roles in cancer cell growth, survival, and spread. These molecules may include proteins, enzymes, or receptors that are overexpressed or mutated in cancer cells.

3. Personalized Treatment:

Targeted therapy is often considered a personalized or precision medicine approach because it takes into account the unique genetic and molecular characteristics of a patient's cancer. This helps to tailor treatment to the individual's specific tumor type and its molecular profile.

4. Combination Therapy:

Targeted therapy can be used alone or in combination with other treatments such as chemotherapy, radiation therapy, or immunotherapy. Combining different types of therapies can enhance their effectiveness and improve outcomes.

5. Side Effects:

While targeted therapy is generally more selective in targeting cancer cells, it can still have side effects. The side effects may be different from those seen with traditional chemotherapy and can vary depending on the specific drug and individual response.

6. Monitoring and Follow-up:

During targeted therapy, patients are closely monitored to assess treatment effectiveness and manage any side effects. Follow-up tests and scans are conducted to track the tumor's response to treatment.

7. Continuous Research:

Targeted therapy is a rapidly evolving field of cancer treatment. New drugs and targeted agents are continually being developed and tested in clinical trials to expand treatment options and improve outcomes for patients.

8. Limitations:

While targeted therapy has shown promising results in certain cancers, it may not be effective for all types of cancer. Some tumors may not have the specific molecular targets that these drugs can act upon.

Bevacizumab (Avastin)

Bevacizumab is a recombinant humanized monoclonal antibody that targets vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). VEGF is a protein that promotes the growth of new blood vessels, a process known as angiogenesis. By blocking VEGF, Bevacizumab inhibits the formation of new blood vessels around the tumor, starving it of nutrients and oxygen, and potentially slowing its growth. Bevacizumab is often used in the treatment of recurrent glioblastoma, a type of aggressive brain tumor.

It is used in the treatment of certain types of brain tumors, such as glioblastoma and anaplastic astrocytoma.

3-Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids, also known as glucocorticoids or steroids, are a class of hormones produced naturally in the adrenal glands. Synthetic versions of these hormones are used as medications to treat various medical conditions, including brain tumors. Corticosteroids have anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive properties, making them effective in managing inflammation and reducing the body's immune response. In the context of brain tumors, corticosteroids are often prescribed to alleviate symptoms and improve the quality of life for patients. Here's some information about their use:

1. Purpose and Benefits:

Corticosteroids are commonly used in brain tumor treatment to reduce brain swelling (edema) caused by the tumor or its treatment. The brain swelling can lead to increased pressure inside the skull, which can cause symptoms like headaches, nausea, vomiting, and changes in consciousness.

By reducing inflammation and edema, corticosteroids can relieve these symptoms and improve neurological function. This can enhance the patient's comfort and overall well-being during the treatment process.

2. Common Corticosteroids Used:

Dexamethasone: This is one of the most frequently prescribed corticosteroids for brain tumor patients. It has potent anti-inflammatory effects and is available in different formulations, including tablets, injections, and intravenous (IV) preparations.

Prednisone: Another corticosteroid used to manage brain tumor-related edema. It is available in oral form and is commonly used in combination with other treatments.

3. Dosage and Administration:

The dosage and duration of corticosteroid treatment depend on the severity of brain swelling and the patient's individual response. The goal is to find the lowest effective dose that provides symptom relief without causing significant side effects.

Corticosteroids are usually administered orally in tablet form, but for severe cases or when the patient cannot take oral medications, IV or intramuscular injections may be used.

4. Side Effects:

While corticosteroids can be effective in managing symptoms, they are not without side effects. Prolonged use or high doses may lead to various side effects, including increased appetite, weight gain, mood changes, fluid retention, high blood pressure, and increased risk of infections.

It's crucial for patients to work closely with their healthcare team to monitor and manage these side effects. In some cases, a gradual tapering of the medication may be necessary to avoid withdrawal symptoms.

5. Precautions:

Corticosteroids may interact with other medications, so it's essential for patients to inform their healthcare providers about all the medications they are taking.

Abruptly stopping corticosteroid treatment can lead to adrenal insufficiency, a condition in which the body is unable to produce enough natural corticosteroids. Therefore, the dosage should be gradually tapered off under medical supervision.
